As They Seem

Open Guest Casting

As They Seem is a Daggerheart campaign in the Chrysallis universe — a live-play podcast where four players (Ayla Potter, Dan Martin, Hannah Anway, and Ben Ellsworth) portray a group of travelers desperately trying to navigate a beautiful and foreign land: The Feywild.

Inspired by A Midsummer Night's Dream and Gaelic mythology, the heroes underwent a strange transformation early in their journey — they were forced to cut up their character sheets and scatter their personality traits to the winds. Those traits are now loose in the world. Lost.

In the Feywild, every creature they meet is a Non-Player Character: a being with one defining quality, no real depth, and a desperate hunger to become more. To have agency. To be real.

The ancient magic that scattered the heroes' traits has the power to do exactly that — to awaken an NPC into a true character, capable of making their own choices and carving their own story.

Those awakened NPCs will be played by you.

Four friends have already walked into this story as surprise guests. Read how it works — then pick from the roles still open.

What You Actually Do

Four guests have already done this. Here’s how it works now — then what they actually did at the table.

01

You Codesign, Then Get a Secret Score

Before recording, you and Nathan shape the character together — desire, tone, how hard to lean. Then you get a short printable secret score (usually ~4 pages): who you are, what you want, how you might win or let go, an entrance cue, and six moves. Moves are sparks with fill-in blanks — not a script and not a character sheet to memorize.

02

You Arrive as a Surprise (Usually)

Most guests hide nearby and walk in mid-session. The main players do not know you’re coming. Sometimes you’re already “in the scene” when they arrive — either way, they meet your character for the first time on mic. Nathan runs the world, map, and numbers. You own the desire, voice, and imagery.

03

You Want Out Loud. Nathan Rules.

When you want to do something, describe it vividly. Nathan invents the ruling live, calls for dice if needed, and keeps Fear / clocks / props honest. You don’t need system knowledge — first-timers have already crushed this. Mid-scene deals you invent are welcome; the score is a menu, not a cage.

04

You Name Yourself — Then Maybe Leave the Table

Your title is on this page; your name is claimed live and enters the podcast forever. If the trait leaves you, a herald (Runs Before Rain) delivers a tiny moral, snaps their fingers — and you go deadpan, stand, and leave the table. No trait = you’re not a player anymore. Comedy and horror in one exit.

How it actually feels at the table

  • You’re designed to lose often — but you must play to win. Earned off-ramps, bargains, and ally paths exist; force is costly and memorable.
  • Division of labor is fixed: you drive desire and wild imagery; Nathan handles dice, map, trackers, and telegraphs.
  • Encounters vary wildly: song duel, sinking manor horror, lightning-pole chase with a public energy bar, or a chaotic ER where you’re the party’s ally. Not every role is a villain.
  • Saturday mornings, ~10am–2pm, recorded for the Heart of the Dice podcast. You’ll get the score a few days ahead.

From the table so far

Four guests. Four totally different vibes. Same job: want something badly and make the room react.

Guest 01 · Hailey

Eleanor — Echoing Bloom

Summer Court banquet · Ennalyn’s mirror · satyr musician

Nobody at the table even knew guest players were a thing. Hailey burst in after a herald verse, named herself Eleanor, and performed her own entrance song. Mid-fight she used The Lariat of Light — a rope of glowing sound from her fiddle that lashed around Ennalyn and pulled a memory of performing with old bandmates down the tether, playing it back in Eleanor’s flatter voice.

Nathan’s line, live: “You just narrated everything you want to do — I’ll come up with the ruling.” Ennalyn’s player rolled to resist. The scene went sideways in the best way. The party eventually reclaimed the trait; Eleanor’s exit was the snap-finger deadpan leave.

Takeaway: describe the drama you want. Nathan makes it real. Surprise is the product.
Guest 02 · Jordan

Holloway — Edmund’s Fetch

Drownmere sinking manor · Edmund’s mirror · quiet horror

Jordan played a Fetch wearing Edmund’s face a half-beat wrong inside a childhood house slowly drowning in quicksand. Mirrors, attic lantern, basement sludge, notes on still-wet walls — the location was the sensory phrase.

Mid-scene, Jordan invented a deal that wasn’t on the score: confess something / bring a childhood doll to the basement figure, and the Lann could go free. Nathan greenlit it live. The trade landed. Then Runs Before Rain: “Holloway must go away…” — snap, deadpan exit — while the house kept sinking without him.

Takeaway: the score is permission, not a cage. Your mid-scene deal can become the ending.
Guest 03 · Denton

Dart — Lightless Sparks

The Pale Ring · Kai’s mirror · toxic Joy Bringer foil

Denton arrived already in the scene (guest-from-start), wrote and performed an entrance rap, named himself Dart, and wore the bit physically. A public energy bar sat on the table while neon lightning hit petrified poles. He leaned hard into uncomfortable foil — LinkedIn-bro positivity, “join me in my field,” trying to replace Kai’s spark with Liira’s warmer brand.

Charge spiked past the designed maximum. The party had to invent a new win: sap the vibe instead of fleeing. At the end, Dart offered the Lann back; Kai’s player refused the last request. The old trait died with him — and an iridescent blank Lann appeared so Kai could rewrite an Experience.

Takeaway: play the dark mirror sincerely and play to win. Even a “win” can become horror — and the table will invent rulings with you.
Guest 04 · Cameron

Dr. A — The Morning Dew

Amberveil field hospital · Edmund’s Quack · ally, not villain

Cameron’s first TTRPG since a 2019 one-shot. He sat in a closet for hours, then burst out as a Red Cap field doctor who’d absorbed Edmund’s care-competence trait. He drafted the party as interns (“find your Stick”), ran a chaotic triage ward, and chased an ally win condition: save everyone.

They did. Patients lived — including a scorched stranger who needed CPR mid-shift. In the finale, Dr. A wrung the moth into Edmund’s hands — you are the doctor now — and faded back to a sketch as the name whisked away.

Takeaway: zero rules knowledge is fine if your desire is loud. Not every guest is an antagonist — some gift the trait back with dignity.

The Masked Lovers
Hosts of the Lonely Soirée at Brimstone Ford
Two voices, a hundred masks, and a romantic dinner that's actually a desperate game of hide-and-seek with their own hearts.
▾
Court Atlas — Field Notes The primary crossing point for all regional travel, maintained with genuine efficiency by a resident fey of considerable social talent. First-time visitors occasionally report agreeing to arrangements they cannot fully recall — this is considered a normal feature of the crossing experience. Carries Words Beyond Sight recommends keeping at least one hand in a pocket throughout all introductions.

Two fey who shared one trait — a desire for connection so overwhelming it never knows when to stop — and the scattering split it clean down the middle. Now one of them can't stop talking, and the other can't speak at all. Both are throwing the same lavish soirée on opposite sides of the same crumbling pavilion at Brimstone Ford, and neither knows the other is here. Each has dressed a dining hall full of porcelain mannequins as "guests" and is desperately, charmingly, a little unhingedly hosting a dinner for no one.

2-player roleplay Whodunnit format Masks & romance

Role 1 — The Overspeaking Host

Every silence terrifies you, so you fill it — toasts, stories, introductions for mannequins who can't respond. You talk so much you've never noticed you're alone.

  • Narrate an entire dinner party single-handedly, assigning the party roles, names, and backstories on the spot.
  • Hand out masks to party members mid-meal — each one changes how this Host perceives and talks to them.
  • Decide, the instant the other lover is found, whether to finally stop talking.

Role 2 — The Silent Guest

You haven't spoken in longer than you can remember. You communicate entirely through gesture, masks, and the positioning of mannequins around the table — a language only your missing partner would recognize.

  • Direct the "other half" of the same dinner using only gesture, mannequin placement, and mask-swaps — no dialogue.
  • Lead party members through a wordless puzzle that's actually a message meant for someone else entirely.
  • Decide, the moment recognition happens, whether you can finally speak again.
Together, You'll...
  • Run two halves of the same dinner party simultaneously — the party moves between rooms, swapping masks, slowly realizing both "hosts" are looking for the same missing person.
  • Decide together the moment the masks come off — and whether reunion plays as triumphant, devastating, or both.

The Maestro & The Second
Keepers of the Keening Scar
One plays the music that bends gravity. The other has never once been allowed to play.
▾
Court Atlas — Field Notes The northern cliff offers the most dramatic views available in the region, with natural stone formations producing sustained musical tones in all wind conditions. A resident wind spirit maintains high standards for visitors, and non-compliant travellers have occasionally been relocated to the cliff base by methods they found difficult to describe. The Scar is best appreciated from a respectful distance on a first visit.

A single trait — devotion to music so total it became a kind of discipleship — split into a master and a student who can never quite become equals. The Maestro, a Winter Eladrin frozen in his coldest season, plays music that bends gravity along the cliff face. His Second has stood beside him for longer than memory, has never been permitted an instrument, and communicates only through hums, gestures, and the wind.

2-player roleplay Combat + roleplay hybrid Gravity-bending music

Role 1 — The Maestro

Cold, commanding, and certain that what you're doing is teaching. The terrain itself answers to your playing.

  • Bend gravity along the cliff edge with your music — redirect a party member's movement mid-action.
  • Silence anyone who performs something unworthy in your presence.
  • Issue an Invitation — if someone genuinely moves you, the whole encounter changes.

Role 2 — The Second

You've never spoken and never played — only hummed, gestured, and watched. Something in you wants to, badly.

  • Communicate entirely through gesture, humming, and the wind itself — no spoken dialogue.
  • Subtly help (or undermine) the Maestro depending on how the party treats you.
  • Decide, mid-scene, whether to finally defy your teacher — and play something of your own.
Together, You'll...
  • Turn the cliffside into a shifting battlefield where gravity, sound, and an old, unequal relationship are all in play at once.
  • Decide together whether this ends as a duel, a lesson, or — for the first time — a duet.
